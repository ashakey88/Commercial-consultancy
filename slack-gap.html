The Slack Gap: the real divide in UK consumer spending isn't rich vs poor

AS
Andrew Shakespeare
June 2026
3 min read

Most cost-of-living commentary measures the wrong thing. It looks at income, or it looks at spend, and draws a line between rich and poor. But ONS spending data reveals something more specific and more useful for anyone selling direct to consumers: a slack gap — the widening difference between what households earn and what they're forced to spend just to function.

That gap, not income alone, is what determines who can absorb a price increase and who can't.

Chart 1: the essentials line is converging — at the worst end

Across the ten UK income deciles, the share of spend going on essentials (housing, food, transport) falls steadily as income rises, while discretionary spend (recreation, dining, non-essential retail) rises in its place. Decile 1 households spend 54% of everything they have on essentials. Decile 10 households spend 42%.

That's a 12-point gap — and it explains why the bottom of the market isn't simply "spending less." They have structurally less room to spend on anything beyond survival, regardless of how prices move.

Chart 2: income outpaces spend fastest at the top — that's the real signal

Here's the cut nobody draws: plot disposable income growth against total spend growth, decile by decile. The bottom decile's income and spend rise together — they spend what they earn, because they have to. By decile 10, income has grown to 11 times the baseline while spend has only grown to 4 times. That gap between the two lines is slack — money that exists but isn't required to go anywhere. It's what allows a household to absorb a price rise without changing behaviour.

The widening space between the blue line and the grey bars is the "slack" — disposable income that isn't tied to required spend.

The top three deciles have slack. Deciles 4 through 7 — squarely "average" UK households — have very little. That's the segment most ecommerce brands assume is comfortably mid-market. The data says they're closer to the edge than the edge itself.

"Slack, not income, predicts price tolerance — and slack is disappearing fastest in the middle of the distribution."

What this means for positioning

This isn't "rich people buy premium, poor people buy cheap." It's that slack, not income, predicts price tolerance — and slack is disappearing fastest in the middle of the distribution, the exact segment most consumer brands are built for.

  • Stop assuming "mid-market" means safe. If your customer sits in deciles 4-7, they have less buffer than their income suggests. A small price increase lands harder than your model assumes.
  • Premium only works with real slack behind it. Deciles 8-10 can absorb price increases without flinching — but only because they have headroom, not because they're simply "wealthier." Price to the slack, not the income bracket.
  • The value end is getting more crowded, not less. As slack disappears mid-market, more households behave like value shoppers — even ones who wouldn't have a year ago. If you're positioned as affordable, expect more competition for that same customer.

The honest takeaway: don't ask "are my customers rich or poor." Ask "how much slack do they actually have left" — because that's the number that decides whether your next price change is safe.
