The retention pyramid: why your email flow isn't the problem

Most founders treat retention as a marketing automation problem. The data says it's a product and proposition problem first — and the gap between the two is bigger than most realise.

AS
Andrew Shakespeare
June 2026
7 min read

Ask a founder how they're going to fix retention and the answer is almost always the same: a better post-purchase email flow. Maybe a loyalty programme. Maybe "subscribe and save." These are the levers that feel controllable — they sit inside Klaviyo, they can be built this sprint, and an agency can sell them to you in a single deck.

They're also, structurally, the smallest levers available. The evidence — both the rigorous academic kind and the messier industry-benchmark kind — points the same way: retention is overwhelmingly a function of what you sell, how it's first experienced, and whether the core proposition naturally invites a second purchase. Lifecycle marketing matters, but it's optimising around the edges of something the product and proposition have to get right first.

The retention pyramid

It helps to think of retention as a pyramid, not a checklist. Each layer depends on the one beneath it — and the layers at the top, the ones founders gravitate to first, are the smallest in actual impact.

The foundation: category economics dwarf everything else

The single clearest piece of evidence for this entire argument is how dramatically retention varies by category, independent of any marketing effort at all. Industry retention benchmarks show grocery and consumables brands retaining 40-65% of customers, while luxury goods retail averages just 9.9%. That's not a gap any email flow closes. It's the proposition itself — one category is bought repeatedly because it's consumed and replaced, the other is often bought once because the need, once met, is met.

This is the uncomfortable truth for a lot of founders: if you've built a brand around a durable, one-time-need product — a piece of furniture, a single capital item, a gift-led purchase — no amount of post-purchase marketing turns that into a high-retention business. The proposition itself sets the ceiling. The right response usually isn't "try harder on email," it's "build or acquire an adjacent product line that gives the customer a real reason to come back."

The structure layer: range and replenishment cycle

Assuming the category allows for repeat purchase at all, the next question is whether your specific range is built to capture it. This is where a lot of single-product or narrow-range D2C brands quietly cap their own retention without realising it. A consumable with a predictable usage cycle — supplements, coffee, skincare — creates a natural reorder point that a brand can plan around. A single durable product with no consumable add-on, accessory line, or natural "next size/next variant" purchase has no structural mechanism for repeat revenue at all, regardless of how good the customer experience was.

Subscription economics make this starkly visible. Subscription retention is highly sensitive to what's actually being bought and at what price point — lower-priced, habitual products sustain dramatically higher retention than higher-priced, more discretionary ones, because the purchase logic is closer to "replenish a need" than "justify a want" each time. The lesson generalises beyond subscriptions: if your range doesn't map to an ongoing need, you're optimising lifecycle marketing around a structural ceiling no campaign can lift.

The experience layer: a real but conditional driver

Once the proposition and range are right, first experience genuinely matters — but it's an amplifier, not a creator, of repeat purchase potential. Customer experience research consistently links onboarding quality to loyalty: a strong majority of customers report being more likely to stay loyal to brands that invest in welcoming, educational post-purchase content, and companies that put experience at the centre of the business report close to double the annual growth in retention and repeat purchase rate compared with those that don't.

The honest caveat: much of this research comes from industry and vendor sources rather than peer-reviewed studies, so treat the precise percentages as directional rather than gospel. But the underlying logic holds up under scrutiny — a confusing unboxing experience, a slow first delivery, or a product that doesn't match expectations on arrival can sour an otherwise retention-friendly category before any lifecycle email gets sent. Experience can't manufacture a reason to repurchase that doesn't exist, but it can absolutely destroy one that did.

"Lifecycle marketing optimises around the edges of a proposition. It cannot manufacture a reason to repurchase that the product itself doesn't provide."

The tactics layer: useful, but the smallest lever

None of this is an argument against email flows, loyalty programmes, or subscribe-and-save. They work — within limits. Automated, behaviourally-triggered email flows reliably outperform generic broadcast campaigns, and well-run loyalty programmes generate genuine incremental revenue through referral and repeat-purchase behaviour. Some of the figures circulating in marketing benchmarks (a handful of vendor reports claim 300%+ revenue increases from segmentation or automation) should be read with real scepticism — they're often vendor-published, self-selected, and not independently verified — but even taking a conservative reading, automation and personalisation clearly deliver a meaningful uplift on top of whatever retention the underlying proposition already supports.

The key word is "on top of." A loyalty programme bolted onto a single-purchase, no-replenishment product is optimising a number that has a low structural ceiling. The same programme bolted onto a consumable, replenishment-led range with a strong first experience has real compounding potential, because it's amplifying something that was already working.

A quick self-check

Before you brief another email flow, ask:
Does my core product get consumed, run out, or wear out — creating a natural reason to reorder?

What this means practically

  • Diagnose before you automate. Before investing in a new flow or loyalty platform, work out what percentage of your customer base has a structural reason to buy again. If that number is low, the highest-leverage fix is product or range, not marketing.
  • Consider an adjacent product line before an email redesign. If your core product is durable and one-time-purchase by nature, the biggest retention lever available might be a consumable add-on, not a better welcome series.
  • Fix the first 30 days before the next 12 months. Onboarding and fulfilment quality determine whether a customer with a genuine reason to return actually does. Audit this before adding more touchpoints to a leaky experience.
  • Use lifecycle marketing to amplify, not rescue. Subscribe-and-save and loyalty programmes are genuinely valuable tools — once the underlying proposition gives them something real to amplify.

The uncomfortable version of this article is simple: if your retention is low, the answer is rarely "send more emails." It's "look honestly at whether what you sell, and how you sell it, gives someone a real reason to come back" — and only then start optimising the campaigns that remind them to.
