Are you a Google brand or a Meta brand?

Most ecommerce founders treat Google and Meta as two budget lines on the same spreadsheet. They're actually two different business models — and most brands are structurally built for one, not both.

AS
Andrew Shakespeare
June 2026
6 min read

Spend enough years running paid media across ecommerce brands and a pattern becomes impossible to ignore. Some brands are built for Google. Some are built for Meta. And the ones that try to be both, evenly, usually underperform on each.

This isn't a platform preference. It's a structural fit between what a brand sells, how it's priced, and the economics each platform demands in return for working with it. Get the fit wrong and you'll spend years fighting your own ad accounts without understanding why nothing quite clicks.

Two different jobs, not two budget lines

The mechanism is well understood at a high level: Google Search captures demand that already exists, while Meta creates demand among people who weren't actively looking. Someone searching a specific product name has already decided what they want — Google's job is simply to be there. Meta's job is harder: interrupt someone mid-scroll and convince them they have a want they hadn't articulated yet.

That difference in job shows up directly in cost. Average Google Search CPCs run roughly three times higher than Meta CPCs, and Google typically converts at a meaningfully higher rate per click because the visitor arrives with intent already formed. Meta's retargeting layer narrows that gap — warm Meta audiences can convert at close to Google Search rates while costing 40-60% less per click — but cold Meta traffic, the kind that actually grows a customer base rather than just harvesting existing demand, plays an entirely different economic game.

The pattern: what makes a brand skew one way

Once you've run enough accounts, the brands that thrive on each platform start to look distinctly different from each other — not in category, but in structure. Here's the pattern as I've seen it play out repeatedly.

Characteristic
Google-leaning brand
Meta-leaning brand
Price position
Competitive, value or mid-market — wins on being findable and well-priced when someone already wants the category
Premium — needs margin to absorb a higher blended CAC from demand creation, not demand capture
Range
Wide — more SKUs means more search terms to own, more long-tail queries, more category coverage
Narrow, curated — a tight, visually coherent range is easier to make scroll-stopping and brand-led
Buyer behaviour
Buyer already knows what they want, searches for it by name or category
Buyer didn't know they wanted it until the ad created the desire
Ceiling
Efficient, but capped by category search volume — once you own your terms, growth requires entering new categories
Far more scalable — audience size isn't the constraint, ad spend and creative supply are
What breaks it
Entering a tight niche with low search volume — you max out efficiency fast with nowhere left to scale
Thin margins — if the product can't absorb cold-traffic CAC, Meta-led growth becomes unprofitable fast

Notice the asymmetry in the "ceiling" row. Google is the more efficient channel almost everywhere, but efficiency and scalability aren't the same thing. A brand selling into a well-defined, smaller category can dominate its search terms quickly — and then has nowhere further to go on that platform. Meta has no such category ceiling. The constraint on Meta isn't audience size, it's whether your margin can fund the cost of creating demand rather than simply capturing it.

"Google rewards being found. Meta rewards being wanted. Those require almost opposite muscles to build."

Why premium economics are non-negotiable on Meta

This is the part founders underweight most. Meta's cold prospecting CAC will always be higher than Google's intent-driven CAC for a directly comparable product, because you're paying to manufacture interest rather than harvest it. The only way that math works long-term is if your margin per unit is high enough to absorb it — which is precisely why so many of the breakout Meta-native brands of the last decade have been premium-positioned: higher AOV, higher margin, often DTC-only so there's no retail price anchor undercutting the story.

A brand with thin unit economics trying to scale primarily through Meta cold prospecting is fighting the platform's fundamental economics, not just its competitors. It's not a creative problem or a targeting problem — it's a structural mismatch between the platform's CAC reality and the brand's margin reality.

→ Google-leaning

→ Meta-leaning

What this means practically

  • Diagnose your platform-fit before diagnosing your campaigns. If a Meta-leaning brand with thin margins is underperforming on Meta, the fix usually isn't creative or targeting — it's pricing and positioning. No amount of campaign optimisation fixes a structural margin problem.
  • If you've maxed out Google, that's not a failure — it's a signal. Hitting the ceiling on your search terms means the category-level efficiency play is largely played out. The next growth phase likely requires building genuine demand, which is Meta's job, not Google's.
  • Don't force range expansion to "become more Google." Widening a curated range to chase more search terms often dilutes the brand coherence that made it work on Meta in the first place. Know which game you're optimising for before you change the product.
  • Most mature brands eventually need both — but sequenced, not blended evenly. Google-led brands often add Meta to build a moat against new entrants. Meta-led brands often add Google to capture the branded search demand their own ads have created. The sequencing matters more than the split.

The honest test isn't "which platform performs better" — it's "which platform's economics match what I'm selling." Get that fit right and the campaigns mostly take care of themselves. Get it wrong and no amount of optimisation will fix a structural mismatch between your margin and the platform's CAC reality.
