The tax you can't see: how black box bidding handed Google and Meta an ever-larger slice of ecommerce

A decade of automation, opacity, and platform consolidation — by the numbers.

AS
Andrew Shakespeare
June 2026
6 min read

Every year, ecommerce brands spend more on Google and Meta ads. Every year, the platforms say the algorithms are getting smarter. And every year, advertisers hand over a little more control in exchange for the promise of better performance.

The numbers bear this out — but not quite in the way the platforms would frame it. Between 2015 and 2025, global ecommerce sales grew roughly fourfold. Over the same period, Google and Meta's combined advertising revenue grew by nearly six times. That gap is, in large part, the story of black box bidding: the systematic removal of advertiser control through automation, and the extraction of an increasingly large share from the ecommerce ecosystem in return.

The growth gap, visualised

Global ecommerce growth has been strong but decelerating since the pandemic peak, settling back toward high single-digit annual increases. Combined Google and Meta ad revenue tells a different story — it has consistently outpaced the ecommerce growth it's supposed to be serving.

Ecommerce grew roughly 4x over the decade. Combined Google and Meta ad revenue grew nearly 6x — from approximately $84 billion in 2015 to around $410 billion in 2025. The gap between those two lines is the implied "ad tax": a growing share of every pound spent in ecommerce flowing back to the two platforms as advertising expenditure, now sitting at an estimated 7%+ of global ecommerce GMV, up from roughly 5.6% in 2015.

The black box timeline

This didn't happen overnight. It was a decade-long, deliberate shift — and one that maps closely onto the acceleration in platform revenue capture.

2010
Enhanced CPC
Google's first step into automated bidding. Advertisers retained control over base bids and structure — automation was an assistive layer, not a replacement.
2016
Smart Bidding formalised
Target CPA, Target ROAS, and Maximise Conversions arrive. The pitch: algorithms see more signals than humans can process. The trade-off: less visibility into why bids were placed.
2018
Smart Shopping — black box enters ecommerce
Google automated both bidding and placement across its properties. For the first time, ecommerce advertisers lost meaningful control over where Shopping ads appeared and how budget was allocated.
Nov 2021
Performance Max launches
The most significant shift in the platform's history. PMax serves ads across every Google channel from a single budget. At launch, it gave virtually no performance breakdown by channel, asset, or audience — advertisers called it the blackest black box in Google's history.
Sep 2022
Smart Shopping retired, PMax made mandatory
All Smart Shopping campaigns were force-upgraded. There was no longer an opt-out from automated, black box campaign management for Google Shopping advertisers.
Late 2022
Meta launches Advantage+ Shopping
A near-identical playbook to PMax: targeting, placement, and bid decisions handed entirely to Meta's algorithm, with limited insight into how budget is allocated.
2023–2026
Consolidation accelerates
PMax and Advantage+ become the dominant campaign types. Resisting automation becomes a competitive disadvantage. Agencies shift from competing on bid management to competing on creative and audience signals — the only levers left inside the box.

Platform concentration today

The consolidation hasn't slowed — if anything, it's intensifying at the very top. Heading into 2026, Meta is on track to overtake Google in global ad revenue for the first time, with both companies still capturing a near-identical, dominant share of the market between them.

Why automation serves the platforms

To be fair, black box bidding isn't purely extractive. Many advertisers have seen genuine performance improvements from automated campaigns, and the algorithms do have access to more signals than any human buyer. But there's a structural asymmetry that gets underreported: the platforms set the rules of the game they also play.

When Google's Performance Max allocates budget across Search, Display, and YouTube simultaneously, it's making a decision that consolidates spend into its own highest-margin inventory. When Meta's Advantage+ algorithm serves retargeting ads to an audience that was already likely to convert, it captures credit for revenue the brand may have generated organically anyway — while spending budget that could have driven incremental growth instead.

Reduced transparency compounds the problem. When advertisers can't see which channels their PMax budget is going to, they can't benchmark the blended return against channel-specific alternatives. Opacity makes it harder to push back, and harder to walk away.

What this means for your brand

  • Don't take blended ROAS at face value. If you can't see the channel-by-channel breakdown, you can't tell whether PMax or Advantage+ is genuinely driving incremental revenue or simply claiming credit for demand you'd have captured anyway.
  • Build independent measurement. Media mix modelling and incrementality testing exist precisely because platform-reported numbers can't be fully trusted in isolation. If you can't verify the platform's numbers, you need your own.
  • Treat automation as a tool, not a strategy. Automated campaigns can still work well — but understand what you're participating in. The algorithm is optimising for the platform's definition of performance, which isn't always the same as yours.

The thesis here isn't that Google and Meta are bad actors, or that automated bidding has made advertising worse across the board. It's simpler and more structural: when platforms control the bidding mechanism, they tend to extract an increasing share of the economic value flowing through their systems. The data bears this out, year after year. The machine is learning. The real question is whether it's learning for you, or for the platform.
