Stakes, not edge: why founders optimise the wrong lever

A website redesign can lift conversion by a few percent. Reviews, price, and brand trust move it by hundreds. Here's the data founders aren't looking at.

AS
Andrew Shakespeare
June 2026
6 min read

Ask most ecommerce founders where to focus next quarter and the answer is almost always the same: the website. A redesign, a new theme, a faster checkout, a homepage refresh. It feels like the controllable lever — the one thing fully inside the business's hands.

The data says something uncomfortable about that instinct. Website-level conversion optimisation works, but it's a small lever next to the ones founders routinely underinvest in: price positioning, product credibility, and brand trust. A usable, fast, well-built website is table stakes — it stops you losing customers. It is not, on its own, what wins them.

How big is a "good" website improvement, really?

Structured conversion rate optimisation programmes genuinely work. The evidence is clear and consistent: individual, well-executed tests typically land in the 12-28% improvement range, and a mature, systematic CRO programme can compound to 25-40% annual uplift. That's real, meaningful, worth doing — and it's the ceiling most agencies and consultants are selling you when they pitch a redesign.

Look at the gap. A well-run A/B test or a year of structured optimisation moves the needle by tens of percent. Displaying reviews moves it by hundreds. The order of magnitude isn't close — and yet founders routinely brief an agency on a homepage redesign before they've fixed a thin review base or unclear pricing story.

Reviews aren't a nice-to-have. They're one of the biggest levers available.

The most rigorous evidence here comes from Northwestern University's Spiegel Research Center, working with real retailer transaction data rather than vendor-marketed case studies. The headline finding: conversion rate on a product page with five or more reviews is, on average, 270% higher than a page with none. Crucially, almost all of that lift comes from the first five to ten reviews — you don't need hundreds, you need a credible base.

The effect gets sharper at higher price points, which matters enormously for premium-positioned ecommerce brands. For lower-priced products, displaying reviews lifted conversion by 190%. For higher-priced products, the same intervention lifted conversion by 380% — roughly double the effect. The more expensive the decision, the more a buyer needs third-party proof before committing.

There's a sharp edge to this too: ratings that are too perfect actually underperform. Purchase likelihood typically peaks at a 4.0–4.7 star average, not 5.0 — buyers read a perfect score as suspicious. A handful of honest, mixed reviews builds more trust than a flawless wall of five-stars.

Price isn't just a number. It's a credibility signal.

Pricing research shows a similarly outsized effect, though it cuts in a more nuanced direction than "cheaper always wins." Price elasticity data shows that low prices increase conversion while high prices suppress it, as expected — but well-targeted discounting on the right products can lift conversion by tens of percentage points against an average baseline, an effect size that dwarfs typical UX interventions. The strategic question isn't simply "should we discount," but whether the price itself is telling a coherent story relative to the product's positioning, its competitors, and the trust signals surrounding it.

This is where price, reviews, and brand trust stop being separate levers and start reinforcing each other. A higher price without reviews is a credibility gap. A higher price with strong reviews and a clear brand story is a premium position that converts at the higher rate the Spiegel data shows. The lever isn't "price" or "reviews" in isolation — it's whether the whole commercial story is coherent.

"A usable website stops you losing customers. It rarely makes you win them. The win comes from being believed."

Brand trust is the multiplier underneath all of it

The broadest and perhaps most important data point: trust now directly influences 92% of buying decisions globally, according to a 2026 PwC Consumer Intelligence study spanning 22,000 respondents across 32 countries — up from 88% previously. Trust isn't a soft, unmeasurable brand metric anymore. It's the dominant variable in almost every purchase.

Storytelling is one of the clearest, most cost-effective ways to build that trust. Messages delivered as stories are up to 22 times more memorable than the same information presented as plain facts. And the commercial payoff compounds over time: customers who form a genuine emotional bond with a brand show a lifetime value 306% greater than those who don't — a number that dwarfs any one-time conversion lift from a website change, because it compounds across every future purchase, not just the first one.

What this means practically

  • Fix your website to remove friction, not to win the sale. Slow load times, broken checkout flows, and confusing navigation actively lose you customers — fix these as hygiene. But don't expect a redesign alone to move conversion by more than single or low double digits.
  • Treat your review base as core infrastructure, not an afterthought. If your products have fewer than five reviews, that's a bigger conversion problem than your homepage layout. Prioritise a review generation strategy before your next design sprint.
  • Audit whether your price tells a coherent story. A price that doesn't match your trust signals creates doubt. Either build the credibility to support a premium price, or price in line with the proof you currently have.
  • Invest in brand story with the same rigour you'd apply to a website sprint. It's harder to measure in a single A/B test, but the lifetime value data suggests it's doing more commercial work than almost anything else available to you.

None of this means stop investing in your website. It means recognising what category of investment it is. A fast, clear, well-built site is the floor — necessary, table stakes, the thing that stops you losing customers you'd otherwise have won. The ceiling, the place where the real commercial upside lives, is in whether people believe you, trust the proof in front of them, and feel the price makes sense. That's not a design problem. It's a credibility problem — and the data says it's a far bigger lever than most founders are pulling.
